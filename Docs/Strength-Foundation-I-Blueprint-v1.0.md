## Deliverable 1 — Program Overview

### Purpose

Strength Foundation I is the entry-point strength program in the Forge Legacy catalog. It is the first program most athletes will ever follow in Forge Legacy, and it is designed with that weight in mind.

Its purpose is threefold: establish the five fundamental barbell movement patterns, build a consistent 3x/week training habit, and create the physiological and neurological base required for long-term strength development. This is not a program about moving heavy weight. It is a program about learning to move correctly — and discovering that consistent, intentional training produces real, visible results.

The design philosophy is movement mastery before load. Athletes who rush past technique in pursuit of numbers will plateau early and lose confidence. Athletes who build patiently in this phase arrive at Strength Foundation II with durable competence across all primary patterns and a natural hunger for more challenge.

### Target Athlete

- No consistent barbell training history, or returning after a significant break (6+ months of inactivity)
- Access to a commercial or private gym with: a barbell, squat rack, bench press, and cable/pulley or equivalent pulling apparatus
- 3 non-consecutive days per week available for training
- 45–65 minutes per session available
- Primary goal is to build functional strength or to begin a serious, structured strength practice

### Excluded Athlete Profiles

| Profile | Reason | Redirect |
|---|---|---|
| 6+ months consistent barbell training | Would be underchallenged; linear progression exhausts quickly | Strength Foundation II or Strength Intermediate I |
| No gym/rack access | Barbell squat is the structural centerpiece; cannot be removed | Bodyweight Foundation or Home Conditioning |
| Injury preventing barbell squat | Program requires bilateral squat-pattern under load | Seek professional guidance; program not suitable |
| Primary goal: hypertrophy | Program is structured for strength, not muscle size | Hypertrophy Foundation |
| Primary goal: conditioning | Program is structured for compound strength, not metabolic work | Athletic Conditioning Foundation |

### Expected Outcomes (8-week completion)

- Technical competency in all five fundamental movement patterns: squat, hinge, horizontal push, horizontal pull, vertical pull
- Measurable load increase on all primary lifts from Week 1 to Week 8
- Consistent 3x/week training habit established across a full 8-week block
- Readiness — physically and mentally — to advance to Strength Foundation II

### Success Criteria

- Minimum 21 of 24 sessions completed
- Load increase documented on squat, hinge, and at least one upper-body push
- Athlete can self-execute setup and working sets on all primary barbell lifts without coaching intervention
- Week 8 working weights exceed Week 1 working weights on every primary lift

## Deliverable 2 — Program Architecture

| Element | Decision | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Duration | 8 weeks | Locked in catalog. Sufficient for the neurological adaptation phase that dominates beginner strength gains. Long enough to establish habit and see meaningful results. Short enough to hold attention and maintain program commitment. |
| Sessions per week | 3 | Locked in catalog. Minimum effective training frequency for full-body beginner strength programs. Provides 48+ hours of recovery between every session. Sustainable for athletes with full-time lives. Research finding 1: 3 days/week full body. |
| Total workouts | 24 | 21 working sessions + 3 deload sessions. Workouts 1–18 (Weeks 1–6), Workouts 19–21 (Week 7 deload), Workouts 22–24 (Week 8 peak). |
| Deload week | Week 7 | Mandatory per PAS-D7/D8 for 7–10 week programs. Penultimate week placement. Research finding 8: Week 7 deload. Dissipates 6 weeks of accumulated fatigue. Allows supercompensation before Week 8 peak. |
| Session duration | 45–65 minutes | Research finding 6. Minimum effective volume approach keeps sessions tight. 45 minutes is achievable in the early weeks. 65 minutes is a hard ceiling — sessions that run longer produce fatigue-degraded movement in the final slots, which is worse than not doing those slots at all. |
| Weekly cadence | 3 non-consecutive days | Mon/Wed/Fri is the standard recommendation. Any 3-day arrangement with a minimum of 1 rest day between sessions is valid. Research finding 7: structural recovery. |

**Workout periodization summary:**

| Phase | Weeks | Sessions | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| Adaptation | 1–3 | 1–9 | Sub-maximal loads, movement learning, habit formation |
| Loading | 4–6 | 10–18 | Full linear progression, progressive load increases |
| Deload | 7 | 19–21 | Reduced volume, maintained/reduced loads, recovery |
| Peak | 8 | 22–24 | Return to full volume, best performance of the program |

Week 8 is deliberately the athlete's strongest week. Linear progression, combined with Week 7 recovery, positions the final three sessions as the highest-performance sessions of the program. This is the experience athletes carry into Strength Foundation II.

## Deliverable 3 — Session Structure

Every session across all 8 weeks follows the same 6-section structural template. This is a deliberate design choice. Structural consistency removes decision fatigue for the beginner. By Week 3, the athlete knows exactly what comes next without thinking. That familiarity becomes a source of confidence.

Variation across the three training days lives in loading emphasis and pattern priority — not in structure.

### Session Template (All Training Days)

| Section | Role | Structural Notes |
|---|---|---|
| WARM_UP | Movement preparation + barbell activation | Required per PAS-D9 for STRENGTH category. Includes general movement prep and barbell warm-up sets for the primary compound. Warm-up sets do not count toward working set totals. |
| Slot 1 — Knee-Dominant Compound | Squat-pattern movement. Present every session. | Loading emphasis varies by day (heavy / moderate-technical / moderate). The squat-pattern slot is never absent. Research finding 4: squat every session. |
| Slot 2 — Hip-Dominant Compound | Hinge-pattern movement. Present 2–3 sessions/week. | Day A: secondary emphasis. Day C: primary emphasis. Day B: light or omitted depending on programming. |
| Slot 3 — Upper Body Push | Horizontal or vertical push. Present every session. | Horizontal push is the primary push pattern (3×/week). Vertical push appears in a supporting role on one day. |
| Slot 4 — Upper Body Pull | Horizontal or vertical pull. Present every session. | Both horizontal and vertical pull receive weekly exposure, with emphasis varying by day. |
| Slot 5 — Core/Stability | Anti-extension or anti-rotation work. Present every session. | Closes the session. 2–3 sets. Does not require a new exercise — same core movement can repeat across all three days. |

**Per-session working set count:** 14–18 working sets
**Exercise count:** 5 main working exercises + warm-up
**PAS-D11 alignment:** 5 exercises (range: 4–6) ✓ | 14–18 sets (range: 15–25) ✓

The warm-up for the primary compound lift (barbell warm-up sets) is built into the WARM_UP section as a protocol, not as separate exercises. The athlete performs progressive barbell warm-up sets before reaching working weight on the first compound slot.

## Deliverable 4 — Weekly Movement Coverage

Three training days (A, B, C) cover all seven primary movement patterns within every training week.

### Weekly Coverage Map

| Pattern | Day A | Day B | Day C | Weekly Freq. | Est. Weekly Sets |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Squat — Knee-Dominant | **PRIMARY** (heavy) | **SECONDARY** (moderate / technical) | **SECONDARY** (moderate) | 3× | 9–12 |
| Hinge — Hip-Dominant | **SECONDARY** | Light / omitted | **PRIMARY** (heavy) | 2–3× | 6–10 |
| Horizontal Push | Supporting | **PRIMARY** | Supporting | 3× | 9–12 |
| Horizontal Pull | **PRIMARY** | Supporting | **SECONDARY** | 3× | 9–12 |
| Vertical Push | — | Supporting (1 slot) | — | 1× | 3–4 |
| Vertical Pull | — | — | Primary or Supporting (1 slot) | 1× | 3–4 |
| Core / Stability | Every session | Every session | Every session | 3× | 6–9 |

**Weekly working set total:** ~45–60 sets across 3 sessions

### Day Character Summary

| Day | Primary Character | Secondary Character |
|---|---|---|
| Day A | Lower — Squat-heavy, Hinge-secondary | Horizontal Pull primary |
| Day B | Upper Push — Horizontal Push primary | Squat secondary (technical), Vertical Push supporting |
| Day C | Lower / Pull — Hinge-heavy, Vertical Pull | Squat secondary, Horizontal Pull secondary |

### Design Rationale

**Squat at 3×/week** is the structural signature of this program. This is not the same squat stimulus three times — it is three different loading contexts for the same movement pattern. Day A is the strength session for the squat. Day B is the technique session. Day C is a moderate-loading consolidation session. This differentiation is what makes 3×/week squat safe and productive rather than accumulative. Research finding 4: squat every session.

**Horizontal push and pull at 3×/week** provides the upper-body compound frequency required to establish pattern competence in the beginner timeframe. Frequent, sub-maximal practice builds movement skill faster than infrequent, heavy sessions.

**Vertical push and vertical pull at 1×/week** is appropriate beginner introduction volume. These patterns are introduced and practiced, not trained for peak development. Volume for these patterns grows in Strength Foundation II. Beginning athletes who have never performed an overhead press or lat pulldown need sessions of introduction, not sessions of intensity.

**Hinge at 2–3×/week** provides enough hip-dominant exposure to build pattern competence without competing with the heavier squat day. Day A uses the hinge as a secondary movement after the squat, which prevents loading conflicts.

## Deliverable 5 — Progression Blueprint

### Progression Model: Linear Progression

**Authority:** PAS-D6 mandates Linear Progression for STRENGTH BEGINNER programs. Research finding 3: linear progression.

Linear progression is the correct model for this athlete population because beginners can increase load every single session without periodization complexity. The nervous system is adapting faster than any other system — and load advances drive further adaptation. Beginners who do not progress every session are leaving the most powerful adaptation stimulus on the table.

### Method

Add a fixed weight increment to each compound lift at the next session upon successful completion of all prescribed sets and reps in the current session.

- Progression is session-to-session, not within a session
- Warm-up sets do not count toward working set rep targets
- Success is defined as: completing all working sets at the full prescribed rep target

### Progression Increments

| Lift Category | Increment per session (success) |
|---|---|
| Lower body compounds (squat pattern, hinge pattern) | +5 lb / 2.5 kg |
| Upper body push (horizontal press, vertical press) | +2.5 lb / 1.25 kg |
| Upper body pull (rows, vertical pull) | +2.5–5 lb / 1.25–2.5 kg |

### Load Advancement Philosophy

Starting weights are sub-maximal — chosen to feel almost easy in the first two sessions. This is intentional and non-negotiable.

Weeks 1–2 are movement mastery weeks. Form is the scorecard, not the load. Athletes who start too heavy compromise the movement learning that this phase is designed to produce. Athletes who start appropriately light will advance rapidly and arrive at challenging weights with technical competence already established.

Rapid early progression is the reward for patience at the start.

From Week 3 onward, linear progression activates at full cadence. Every session is a new personal record on every lift. This is the defining experience of a well-run linear progression program — and it is the most powerful motivational engine available to a beginner athlete.

### Failure Handling Philosophy

Two-stage protocol:

1. **First failure** (failed to complete all sets at prescribed reps at a given weight): Repeat the same weight at the next session. Do not deload. One failure does not indicate a true ceiling — it may indicate insufficient rest, a bad night of sleep, or minor accumulated fatigue.

2. **Second consecutive failure** on the same lift at the same weight: Reduce that lift's load by 10% and restart linear progression from the new weight.

This protocol respects the athlete's time and gives genuine failures a clear path forward without being overly reactive to single bad sessions.

Note: Failure-triggered deloads on individual lifts are independent of the Week 7 structural deload. They can co-occur without conflict.

### Movement Mastery vs. Load Advancement

Per research finding 9 (movement mastery before load) and the Coaching Principles Charter:

In the Adaptation Phase (Weeks 1–2), load advancement may be withheld when movement quality is severely compromised. This is a judgment applied during exercise authoring — it is a coaching principle, not an automated system rule. The program should include notes that direct athletes to prioritize movement quality over numbers in the opening weeks.

From Week 3 onward, load advancement is the primary driver and should not be withheld unless form breakdown is severe and visible.

### Deload Interaction

The Week 7 structural deload reduces volume (40–50% set reduction per PAS-D8), not load. Loads are maintained or reduced 10–15% during the deload to allow active recovery without complete detraining.

**Week 8 progression protocol:**
- Athlete returns to pre-deload working weights at the start of Week 8
- Linear progression continues from those weights
- A lift that stalled in Week 6 should restart from a 10% reduction in Week 8 (not from the pre-deload weight)
- The deload provides the recovery necessary to push through previous stall points — athletes often break through in Week 8 what they failed on in Week 6

| Weeks 4–6 (Loading) | 48–54 sets |
| Week 7 (Deload) | 22–30 sets (40–50% reduction, PAS-D8) |

### Per-Session Set Targets

| Phase | Working Sets / Session |
|---|---|
| Weeks 1–3 | 14–17 sets |
| Weeks 4–6 | 16–18 sets |
| Week 7 (deload) | 8–12 sets |
| Week 8 | 16–18 sets |

### Set Distribution Template (Per Session)

| Slot | Working Sets |
|---|---|
| Primary Compound (+ 2–3 warm-up sets, uncounted) | 3–4 sets |
| Secondary Compound | 3–4 sets |
| Third Main Compound | 3 sets |
| Support Pattern (push or pull) | 2–3 sets |
| Core / Stability | 2–3 sets |
| **Session Total** | **13–17 working sets** |

### PAS-D11 Alignment

- Exercises per session: 5 (PAS-D11 range for STRENGTH: 4–6) ✓
- Sets per session: 13–18 (PAS-D11 range for STRENGTH: 15–25) ✓
- Sits at lower range of PAS-D11 intentionally — see rationale below

### Volume Rationale

This blueprint adopts minimum effective volume as the guiding principle. Research finding 5.

**Why minimum effective volume for this program:**

1. Beginners respond to novel stimulus. The training stress required to produce adaptation in a beginner is far lower than the stress required to produce adaptation in an intermediate athlete. High volume is not necessary; appropriate volume at the right frequency is.

2. Low volume leaves headroom for linear progression. Linear progression is the primary adaptation driver. It only functions cleanly when the athlete is not pre-fatigued from excessive volume. Volume and linear load progression compete for recovery resources — minimum effective volume allows both to coexist.

3. Session duration is protected. Minimum effective volume keeps sessions within the 45–65 minute constraint. Sessions that routinely exceed 65 minutes produce declining movement quality in the final slots and reduce the probability of session completion over time.

4. Per research finding 10 (consistency before optimization): A 45-minute session completed every time is worth more than a 75-minute session completed irregularly. The volume question for this program is not "how much produces the best result?" — it is "how much keeps athletes showing up?"

## Deliverable 7 — Recovery Blueprint

### Rest-Day Placement

- 3 non-consecutive training days per week
- Minimum 1 full rest day between each training session
- Preferred cadence: Mon/Wed/Fri — leaves Saturday and Sunday as consecutive full recovery days
- The program does not prescribe specific weekdays. Athletes self-select within the non-consecutive constraint.
- Research finding 7: structural recovery.

### Intra-Session Rest Periods

| Slot Type | Rest Period |
|---|---|
| Primary and secondary compounds | 2–3 minutes |
| Third main compound and support patterns | 90 seconds – 2 minutes |
| Core / stability work | 60–90 seconds |

Full rest between compound sets is non-negotiable. Compressed rest periods reduce the quality of the subsequent set, producing fatigue-driven technique errors rather than the clean reps that build movement competency.

### Fatigue Management

- Sub-maximal starting loads delay the accumulation of heavy-load fatigue to Weeks 4–6
- Volume cap per session prevents fatigue-driven form degradation late in the session
- Consistent rest periods between sets allow full phosphocreatine recovery
- Linear progression means absolute loads remain manageable through the adaptation phase
- 48–72 hours between sessions is sufficient recovery for this audience at this volume

### Beginner Recovery Assumptions

Beginners recover faster between training sessions than intermediate or advanced athletes because absolute loads are lower and total systemic stress per session is lower. This is why 3×/week full-body programming is appropriate here — and why it becomes less appropriate as athletes advance.

However, beginners also accumulate neurological fatigue from learning new movement patterns. The cognitive and coordination demands of skill acquisition are real. Sessions 1–4 will likely produce notable delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS) as the body responds to unaccustomed loading. This is expected, normal, and not a sign of excessive volume. Athletes should be coached to expect this and to continue training through it.

Sleep and nutrition are foundational recovery inputs, but they are outside the scope of this program.

### Week 7 Deload — Rationale

By the end of Week 6, the athlete has completed 18 progressive working sessions under linear load advancement. Accumulated systemic fatigue, muscular fatigue, and neurological fatigue begin to mask fitness gains. The athlete may feel like they are plateauing or regressing — but the fitness is there, hidden under fatigue. The deload is how it is revealed.

**Deload week protocol:**
- Same 3 training sessions (frequency maintained per PAS-D8)
- 40–50% set volume reduction (PAS-D8)
- Loads maintained or reduced 10–15% from working weights
- Same movement patterns — technique is practiced, not stressed
- Same structural template — no variation introduced

**Why supercompensation makes Week 8 the peak:**

Supercompensation does not occur during training. It occurs during recovery. The deload week is the recovery period that allows the body to consolidate the adaptations built across Weeks 1–6. When Week 8 begins, the athlete is rested, the nervous system is fresh, and the linear progression picks up from where it stalled. Most athletes push personal records in all primary lifts during Week 8.

This makes the final three sessions of the program the most powerful sessions — and it sends athletes into Strength Foundation II at the highest point of their current capacity.

## Deliverable 9 — Blueprint Stress Test

### Recovery Risk: LOW–MODERATE → MANAGED

**Concern:** Squat 3×/week accumulates significant quad, knee, and lower-back fatigue over an 8-week block.

**Analysis:** The risk is real but mitigated by loading differentiation. Day A (Primary — heavy) is the only true strength session for the squat. Day B (Secondary — moderate/technical) uses lower loads with a form focus, not a load focus. Day C (Secondary — moderate) sits between the two. The athlete never squats heavy twice in the same week.

Sub-maximal starting weights delay heavy loading to Weeks 4–6. Week 7 deload is the structural release valve for any accumulation that builds in that final working block.

**Critical condition:** If squat loading is not clearly differentiated across the three days during exercise authoring, this risk re-emerges as HIGH. The blueprint must be honored in exercise prescription.

**Status: MANAGED** — contingent on differentiated loading enforcement in authoring.

---

### Adherence Risk: LOW → MANAGED

**Concern:** Beginners have documented high dropout rates in Weeks 2–4.

**Analysis:** Three mechanisms protect adherence here.

First, sessions fit real schedules. 45–65 minutes is an honest and achievable time commitment. Athletes who feel a program respects their time show up consistently.

Second, linear progression delivers visible wins every session in the early weeks. Adding weight to the bar every workout is the most powerful motivational feedback a beginner can receive. The program is designed to make this visible and frequent.

Third, structural simplicity removes decision fatigue. The athlete does not need to think about what comes next. They know the template. They walk in and execute. Familiarity becomes confidence by Week 3.

**Status: MANAGED**

---

### Complexity Risk: LOW → MANAGED

**Concern:** Five movement patterns is a large simultaneous skill acquisition load for a beginner.

**Analysis:** The mitigation is repetition, not reduction. The same five structural slots appear in every session. The same patterns are practiced every week. Warm-up sets for the primary compound double as movement learning reps before working weight is applied.

New patterns are never introduced mid-program. The athlete is not learning a new skill every week — they are deepening five skills over 24 sessions. This is the most effective way to build movement competency in the beginner timeframe.

**Status: MANAGED**

---

### Volume Risk: LOW → MANAGED

**Concern:** Risk in both directions — too little volume = insufficient adaptive stimulus; too much = overtraining and dropout.

**Analysis:** 14–18 sets/session sits at the lower range of PAS-D11 (15–25 for STRENGTH). This is intentional. Beginners produce meaningful adaptation from low volume because every session is a novel stimulus. The downside risk of volume excess (fatigue accumulation, session length creep, dropout) outweighs the marginal upside of higher volume at this level.

The risk here leans toward "too little" rather than "too much." That is the acceptable lean for this athlete profile. Research finding 5: minimum effective volume.

**Status: MANAGED**

---

### Progression Risk: LOW–MODERATE → MANAGED

**Concern:** Linear progression stalls typically emerge in Weeks 5–8 for beginners, with upper-body lifts stalling before lower-body lifts.

**Analysis:** The deload architecture addresses this directly. Week 7 is placed precisely to interrupt accumulated fatigue before it converts stalls into confidence losses. Athletes who stall in Week 6 on upper-body lifts will often break through in Week 8 after the deload.

The failure handling protocol (repeat once, then reduce 10%) provides a clear path for stalls that occur before Week 7. Athletes know what to do and don't need to interpret the stall as failure.

**Important note for exercise authoring:** The program should include coaching language that normalizes late-program stalls on upper-body lifts. Stalls in Weeks 5–6 on the press or row are expected, not concerning. The deload is the solution.

**Status: MANAGED**

## Deliverable 10 — Final Blueprint Recommendation

### Strengths

1. **Research alignment is complete.** Every structural decision traces directly to one or more of the 10 locked benchmark findings. No decisions were made outside the evidence base.

2. **PAS compliance is complete.** PAS-D6 (Linear Progression for STRENGTH BEGINNER), PAS-D7/D8 (Week 7 mandatory deload), PAS-D9 (WARM_UP required for STRENGTH), and PAS-D11 (4–6 exercises, 15–25 sets) are all honored.

3. **Squat-every-session is implemented correctly.** Three different loading contexts — not three identical sessions — mean the squat frequency is productive rather than accumulative.

4. **Minimum effective volume protects adherence and enables linear progression simultaneously.** These two goals do not conflict when volume is managed correctly. This blueprint manages it correctly.

5. **Session structure is identical every day.** This is the most adherence-favorable design for a beginner program. Predictability becomes confidence.

6. **Week 7 deload serves two purposes.** It resolves accumulated fatigue (structural) and introduces the deload concept to athletes who have never experienced a formal recovery week (educational). Both outcomes are valuable.

7. **Succession readiness is specific and testable.** Strength Foundation II does not receive a vague handoff. It receives an athlete with documented competencies, a proven adherence pattern, and a clear expectation of increased challenge.

8. **Sort Order 1 and Featured status are justified.** This is the most broadly accessible program in the Forge Legacy catalog. It makes the fewest assumptions about prior training history and the most reasonable demands on time and equipment. It deserves the front position.

### Weaknesses

1. **Vertical push and vertical pull receive 1×/week.** This is appropriate for beginner introduction but below the pattern exposure of horizontal push and pull. Athletes who want to develop overhead pressing strength or vertical pulling strength will need to continue into Foundation II. The blueprint should not overstate competency in these patterns at graduation.

2. **GYM-only scope excludes a real segment of athletes.** The barbell squat is non-negotiable for this program. Athletes without rack access are excluded, not accommodated. This is the correct decision, but it is a real limitation in the catalog.

3. **Hinge frequency is asymmetric with squat frequency.** Squat appears 3×/week; hinge appears 2–3×/week. This is intentional — competing heavy squat and heavy hinge days on the same frequency would accumulate posterior chain fatigue rapidly. But athletes who specifically want to develop their hip hinge may feel under-served by this ratio.

### Remaining Risks

1. **Squat loading differentiation must be enforced in exercise authoring.** The blueprint requires that Day A squat and Day B/C squat carry meaningfully different loads. If authoring treats all three squat slots at equivalent intensity, the recovery risk re-emerges. This is the single highest-priority constraint to carry into exercise prescription.

2. **Late-program upper-body stalls require coaching language.** Athletes who stall on the bench press or row in Weeks 5–6 need to understand this is normal, expected, and handled. Without that context, stalls become discouragement. Coaching notes in exercise authoring must address this.

3. **Athletes with knee pathology.** Three-session-per-week squat, even differentiated, is contraindicated for some knee conditions. A program-level note directing these athletes to professional guidance is required.

### Confidence Score

**9.0 / 10**

The 0.5 deduction reflects the dependency on differentiated squat loading being correctly enforced during exercise authoring — a constraint this blueprint establishes but cannot guarantee. The 0.5 deduction reflects the known limitation that vertical push and vertical pull receive minimal weekly volume. Neither deduction reflects a structural flaw in the blueprint itself.
